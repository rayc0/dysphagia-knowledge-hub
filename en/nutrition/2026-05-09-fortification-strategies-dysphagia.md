## Introduction

Patients with dysphagia face a paradox: the texture modifications essential for safe swallowing often reduce the energy and nutrient density of their diet. Volume limitations, palatability changes, and reduced appetite further compound nutritional risk. Food fortification — the deliberate addition of energy- or nutrient-dense ingredients to existing foods — offers a practical, patient-centred solution that does not require increasing portion sizes.

This guide provides dietitians with a systematic, evidence-based framework for food fortification in dysphagia management across clinical and community settings.

## Understanding the Fortification Imperative

Studies consistently demonstrate that patients consuming texture-modified diets (TMDs) achieve only 60–75% of estimated energy requirements and 50–70% of protein targets. Pureed and minced diets dilute macronutrient density because water or thickening agents replace naturally calorie-dense food structures. Without active intervention, cumulative energy deficits lead to protein-energy malnutrition, loss of lean body mass, delayed recovery, and increased mortality.

The IDDSI (International Dysphagia Diet Standardisation Initiative) framework provides standardised texture descriptors but does not prescribe fortification; this remains the dietitian's domain.

## Core Fortification Principles

**Preserve IDDSI compliance.** Any additive must be fully incorporated without altering the tested and approved texture level. Lumpy or incompletely dissolved fortifiers can create aspiration risk or choking hazards.

**Favour energy- and protein-dense additions.** Target foods that deliver maximum calories or protein per gram added, minimising volume burden.

**Minimise flavour and appearance disruption.** Patient acceptance is a primary driver of intake. Bland, neutral additives are preferable; flavour-matched options should be chosen when possible.

**Monitor osmolality.** High-protein or high-sugar additions can increase osmolality, contributing to diarrhoea in tube-fed patients or those with compromised gastrointestinal function.

## Macronutrient Fortification Strategies

### Energy Fortification

**Glucose polymers (maltodextrin):** Provide 3.8–4 kcal/g, dissolve readily in hot and cold foods, and are virtually tasteless. Suitable for beverages, soups, and pureed dishes. Typical dose: 5–10 g per serve.

**Vegetable oils and emulsified fats:** Canola, olive, or sunflower oil contribute 9 kcal/g. Incorporate into pureed vegetables or soups. Ensure thorough blending to maintain IDDSI compliance. Medium-chain triglyceride (MCT) oil is particularly useful for patients with fat malabsorption.

**Butter and margarine:** 7 kcal/g; melt readily into hot purees. Enhance palatability and fat-soluble vitamin bioavailability.

**Cream and full-fat dairy:** Fortify custards, soups, and mashed potatoes. Double cream provides approximately 4 kcal/mL and blends smoothly into purees.

**Commercial modular energy supplements:** Products such as Calogen (fat emulsion) or Polycal (carbohydrate powder) are purpose-designed for clinical fortification with predictable caloric delivery.

### Protein Fortification

**Skimmed milk powder (SMP):** 35 g protein per 100 g; dissolves in hot liquids. Add 15–20 g to soups, custards, or mashed potato per serve. Cost-effective and widely available.

**Whey protein isolate or concentrate:** Higher protein fraction (80–90%), rapid digestibility, and leucine-rich amino acid profile. Optimal for stimulating muscle protein synthesis. Use 15–25 g per serve.

**Casein-based supplements:** Slow-release protein appropriate for overnight tube feeding or patients requiring prolonged amino acid availability.

**Soy or pea protein isolates:** Plant-based options for patients with dairy intolerance. Match texture and ensure complete dissolution before serving.

**Egg white powder:** High biological value protein; incorporate into savory purees or baked goods adapted to IDDSI levels.

## Micronutrient Fortification

Energy and protein fortification often displace micronutrients proportionally. Assess baseline intake against dietary reference values (DRVs) and supplement targeted nutrients rather than relying solely on food sources:

- **Vitamin D:** Frequently deficient in institutionalised or housebound dysphagic patients. Supplement 800–2000 IU/day as liquid drops added to food or drink.
- **Calcium:** If dairy avoidance reduces intake, calcium carbonate or citrate powder added to fortified foods.
- **Zinc:** Critical for wound healing and immune function; deficiency is common. Consider zinc sulphate solution or dissolvable tablets.
- **Iron:** Monitor carefully in post-surgical or chronic disease populations; fortify cautiously to avoid constipation.

## Practical Fortification Protocols

### Breakfast
- Porridge or semolina: add 20 g SMP + 10 g maltodextrin + 5 mL MCT oil → +250 kcal, +7 g protein
- Yogurt (smooth, IDDSI Level 6): stir in 15 g whey protein isolate → +12 g protein, +60 kcal

### Lunch and Dinner
- Pureed soups: add 30 mL double cream + 10 g SMP + 5 mL canola oil → +180 kcal, +3 g protein per bowl
- Mashed potato (IDDSI Level 4): add full-fat milk, butter, and 15 g SMP → +150 kcal, +5 g protein

### Beverages
- Fortified milkshakes: full-fat milk + 25 g whey protein + 20 g maltodextrin + ice cream blended smooth → ~450 kcal, 25 g protein per 300 mL

## Monitoring and Adjustment

Reassess nutritional status every 1–2 weeks in acute settings and monthly in community care. Track:

- Body weight (or mid-arm circumference if weighing is impractical)
- Hand grip strength (where feasible)
- Biochemical markers: albumin, prealbumin, CRP, zinc, vitamin D
- Oral intake records and food satisfaction surveys

Adjust fortification intensity based on weight trajectory and appetite changes. Excessive fortification in patients with recovering appetite can cause GI discomfort; taper as oral intake increases.

## Integration with Oral Nutritional Supplements (ONS)

Fortification should complement, not replace, ONS when indicated. Use the stepped approach:

1. Dietary counselling and food-first fortification
2. Add modular supplements (energy/protein powders)
3. Prescribe complete ONS (1.5–2.0 kcal/mL sip feeds)
4. Consider supplemental or total enteral nutrition if oral intake remains inadequate despite steps 1–3

## Documentation and Team Communication

Record fortification strategies in the nutrition care plan, clearly specifying IDDSI level, per-serve additions, target energy/protein, and review frequency. Communicate with kitchen staff and caregivers using simple visual guides. Standardise recipes to reduce preparation variability.

## Conclusion

Food fortification is a cornerstone intervention in dysphagia nutrition care. A systematic, evidence-informed approach — selecting appropriate modular nutrients, maintaining IDDSI compliance, and monitoring outcomes — enables clinicians to close the energy-protein gap without increasing volume burden or swallowing risk. Dietitians are best positioned to lead fortification planning within the multidisciplinary team.
